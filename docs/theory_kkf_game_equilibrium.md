# Theoretical Framework: Koopman Game Theory Equilibrium in Asset Pricing

This document outlines the theoretical path for defining a continuous-time equilibrium in a market where agents (specifically the Market Maker and/or the Insider) utilize the Koopman Operator (via Signature Koopman Kalman Filters) to solve their respective filtering and control problems.

## 1. Classical Setup (Back 1992, 2004)

In classical continuous-time Kyle/Back models:

- **Market Maker (MM)**: Observes aggregate order flow $Y_t = X_t + Z_t$. Sets price $P_t = E[v | \mathcal{F}^Y_t]$.
- **Insider**: Observes true value $v$ and noise trades $Z_t$ (or equivalently infers them). Maximizes expected terminal wealth $E[\int_0^1 (v - P_t) dX_t]$.

The equilibrium is a pair $(H, X)$ where $P_t = H(Y_t, t)$ is a rational pricing rule (satisfying the MM's filtering problem) and $X_t$ is the insider's optimal response (satisfying the Insider's HJB equation) given $H$.

**Crucially, in the standard rational expectations equilibrium, the Insider is assumed to perfectly know the mathematical functional form of the MM's pricing rule $H$.**

---

## 2. Introducing the Koopman Operator

When priors are non-Gaussian (e.g., multimodal distribution of $v$), $H(Y_t, t)$ becomes a highly nonlinear function of the path history of $Y$. Deriving closed-form equilibria is often impossible.

Instead, we propose that the MM uses a **Signature Koopman Kalman Filter (Sig-KKF)**. The MM lifts the observation path to feature space $Z_t = \Psi(Y_{[0,t]})$ and tracks the conditional expectation in this linear feature space using a learned Koopman operator $\mathcal{K}_{mm}$.

### 2.1 The Market Maker's Koopman Pricing Rule

The MM's pricing rule is no longer an analytical function, but rather the output of their filter:

1. $dZ_t = f(Z_t) dt + g(Z_t) dY_t$ (Signature evolution equation in continuous time via Chen's identity/Stratonovich integration).
2. The MM computes $\hat{v}_t = w_{mm}^T \hat{Z}_t$, where $\hat{Z}_t$ is the filtered state utilizing the Koopman Generator $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$ to predict forward.

Therefore, the pricing rule is parameterized by the MM's Koopman model: $P_t = \mathcal{H}(Y_{[0,t]} ; \mathcal{L}_{mm}, w_{mm})$.

### 2.2 The Insider's Problem: To Know or Not to Know?

This brings us to the core theoretical question: **Does the Insider know the MM's update rule (i.e., do they know $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$ and $w_{mm}$)?**

We can define the equilibrium under two distinct informational assumptions:

#### Approach A: Strong Rational Expectations (Koopman Rationality)

_Assumption: The Insider perfectly knows the finite-dimensional Koopman approximation used by the MM._

- **Setup**: The Insider knows that the MM maps $Y_t \to Z_t$ and prices via $P_t = w_{mm}^T Z_t$.
- **HJB Problem**: The Insider's state space must now include the MM's lifted variables $Z_t$. The Insider's HJB is formulated in the finite-dimensional signature space.
- **Equilibrium**: A fixed point where the MM's optimal estimation operator $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$ optimally filters the order flow generated by an Insider who optimally controls $X_t$ _while fully anticipating_ the dynamics of $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$.
- **Pros**: Theoretically rigorous extension of Back (1992). Enables bounded-rationality equilibria where the finite truncation of the signature dictates the exact limits of the equilibrium.
- **Cons**: Extremely difficult to solve explicitly, as the HJB is over a potentially high-dimensional state space $Z_t$.

#### Approach B: Adaptive/Learning Game (Bounded Reality)

_Assumption: The Insider does not perfectly know the MM's internal operator, but builds their own Koopman surrogate of the price impact._

- **Setup**: The Insider observes prices $P_t$ as they trade. They learn their own proxy Koopman operator for price impact: $dP_t \approx \mathcal{L}_{insider}(P_t, X_t)$.
- **HJB Problem**: The Insider continuously solves an adaptive Koopman-MPPI or Koopman-SDRE control problem based on their online estimate of $\mathcal{L}_{insider}$.
- **Equilibrium**: Defined as the stationary limit $t \to \infty$ of a repeated game (or a long-horizon continuous game) where both agents are simultaneously learning. The MM updates $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$ to better filter $v$, while the Insider updates $\mathcal{L}_{insider}$ to better hide their trades.
- **Pros**: Reflects the actual mechanics of reinforcement learning and adaptive algorithms in complex markets. Circumvents needing an analytic solution to the massive HJB.
- **Cons**: It is a purely empirical equilibrium (the "Nash Equilibrium of Learning Algorithms").

## 3. Mathematical Formulation of the Two Paradigms

Given recent literature, we can formalize both approaches to understand their implications.

### 3.1 Paradigm A: Koopman Rationality (Infinite Regress of Expectations)

Following Nimark (2017) on "Dynamic Higher Order Expectations", if the Insider knows the MM is using a Koopman Operator, we enter an infinite regress.
Because the MM filters order flow $Y_t$, the MM's estimate of the value $\hat{v}_t$ depends on their estimate of the Insider's strategy $X_t$. The Insider, in turn, must estimate the MM's estimate of their strategy, ad infinitum.

**The Koopman Solution to Infinite Regress:**

There are two potential ways to resolve this infinite regress mathematically using Koopman theory:

#### Method 1: The LQR Truncation

1. **The Hierarchy**: Let $Z_t^{(k)}$ be the $k$-th order expectation of the signature features. $Z_t^{(0)}$ is the true state, $Z_t^{(1)}$ is the MM's estimate, $Z_t^{(2)}$ is the Insider's estimate of the MM's estimate, etc.
2. **The Truncated State Space**: Nimark demonstrates that under certain conditions, this hierarchy can be truncated into a finite-dimensional state $\mathbf{Z}_t = [Z_t^{(0)}, Z_t^{(1)}, \dots, Z_t^{(K)}]^T$.
3. **The Control Problem**: The Insider's optimal order flow $\theta_t = dX_t/dt$ is derived by solving an HJB equation over this expanded, yet finite-dimensional, state vector $\mathbf{Z}_t$.
   $$ \min*{\theta} E \left[ \int_0^1 (v - w*{mm}^T Z_t^{(1)}) \theta_t dt \right] $$
   Because the Koopman operator linearizes the filter, this massive HJB over the expectation hierarchy reduces to a tractable **Linear-Quadratic structure (LQR)** in the lifted space.

#### Method 2: Fixed Points in Operator Theory (The Exact Solution)

Rather than truncating and solving a massive LQR problem, we can look for true analytical fixed points using Operator Theory.

1. **The Joint Iterated Expectations Operator**: Let $\mathcal{E}$ be the joint expectations operator that maps the current hierarchy of beliefs to the next order. Given the MM's Koopman generator $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$ and the Insider's observation of the true state and order flow, we can construct a joint operator matrix $\mathcal{H}_{joint}$ acting on the Koopman features $Z_t$:
   $$ \mathbf{Z}_t^{(k+1)} = \mathcal{H}_{joint}(\mathcal{L}_{mm}, \mathcal{L}_{ins}) \mathbf{Z}_t^{(k)} $$
   This operator encapsulates \_both_ the MM's filtering update and the Insider's expectation of that update.
2. **Koopman Eigenfunctions**: In the Koopman framework, if we can identify the eigenfunctions (or invariant subspaces) of this joint iterated expectation operator $\mathcal{H}_{joint}$, the infinite regress might trivially converge or be representable analytically.
3. **The Fixed Point (and Uniqueness Considerations)**: We seek a fixed point where applying the joint expectation operator yields a stationary dynamic belief structure (i.e., identifying the principal invariant subspace of $\mathcal{H}_{joint}$).
   **Crucial caveat on uniqueness**: Finding a fixed point in the finite-dimensional Koopman space proves the existence of _an_ equilibrium relative to the chosen basis dictionary. It does _not_ imply that this is the globally unique equilibrium of the underlying nonlinear continuous-time game. In fact, if the true prior is non-Gaussian, the true game may possess multiple nonlinear equilibria. Our truncation guarantees a locally unique "best-fit" equilibrium projected onto the chosen signature basis, but altering the dictionary could unveil other valid equilibrium manifolds.

If this invariant subspace exists and is stable, the Insider does not need to simulate the full hierarchy; they simply project their state onto the exact fixed-point Koopman eigenfunctions and solve a dramatically lower-dimensional HJB.

### 3.2 Paradigm B: Bounded Reality (The Adaptive Control Game)

In a real-world high-frequency market, traders do not compute infinite regresses of rationality. Instead, they operate under "Bounded Reality," acting as reinforcement learners reacting to observed price impact.

**The Adaptive Surrogate Model:**

1. **The MM's Filter**: The MM runs their Sig-KKF: $dZ^{mm}_t = \mathcal{L}_{mm} Z^{mm}_t dt + g_{mm} dY_t$, setting $P_t = w_{mm}^T Z^{mm}_t$.
2. **The Insider's Learning**: The Insider **does not know** $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$ or $Z^{mm}_t$. They only observe $P_t$ and their own trades $X_t$. They construct a surrogate Koopman model of price impact:
   $$ d\hat{Z}^{ins}_t = \mathcal{L}_{ins}(t) \hat{Z}^{ins}_t dt + g_{ins} (\theta*t dt) $$
   where $P_t \approx w*{ins}^T \hat{Z}^{ins}\_t$.
3. **Online Adaptive Control**: The Insider continually updates $\mathcal{L}_{ins}(t)$ using least-squares (RLS) on the observed $(X_t, P_t)$ pairs. Concurrently, they solve a Koopman-SDRE control problem using the _current_ estimate $\mathcal{L}_{ins}(t)$ to find the optimal trading rate $\theta_t$.

**Equilibrium definition:**
The equilibrium is not analytically solved via a fixed point theorem. Instead, it is an empirical stochastically stable state where:

- $\mathcal{L}_{mm}$ optimally filters the order flow generated by the Insider.
- $\mathcal{L}_{ins}$ optimally describes the price impact generated by the MM.
- Both operators are updated online and converge to stationary distributions (e.g., as $t \to \infty$ in a repeated game setting, or exhibiting stationary fluctuations in continuous time).

### 3.3 Why SDRE is Not Just an Approximation in Koopman Space

A common critique of SDRE in standard nonlinear control is that it is merely a sub-optimal approximation of the true Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation. This is because standard SDRE relies on an arbitrary factorization of the nonlinear dynamics $f(x) = A(x)x$, leading to an "optimality gap" where the HJB residual is strictly non-zero.

**However, in the Koopman framework, the SDRE optimality gap vanishes.**

By definition, the Koopman Operator $\mathcal{K}$ provides a _structurally exact_ linear representation of the nonlinear dynamics in the lifted observable space $\mathbf{Z} = \Psi(X)$.

1. **The Unique Generator**: Because the continuous-time Koopman Generator $\mathcal{L}$ governs the rigorous evolution $d\mathbf{Z}_t = \mathcal{L} \mathbf{Z}_t dt$, the factorization $\mathbf{Z}_t \to \mathcal{L} \mathbf{Z}_t$ is unique and globally structurally consistent, unlike arbitrary $A(x)$ choices in standard SDRE.
2. **Solving the True HJB**: When we apply the Riccati equation locally to the Koopman state $\mathbf{Z}_t$, it is not an ad-hoc approximation. As the dictionary size approaches infinity (e.g., the complete Path Signature), the linear-quadratic value function in the lifted space $V(\mathbf{Z}) = \mathbf{Z}^T P \mathbf{Z}$ perfectly spans the true, highly nonlinear value function $V(X)$ in the original state space.
3. **Zero Optimality Gap**: The "Factorization Gap" that plagues classical SDRE is eliminated by the Koopman lift. Therefore, Koopman-SDRE is not a heuristic; it is a convergent numerical method for exactly solving the intractable nonlinear HJB equation.

## 4. Extension: The Multi-Insider Game ($N > 1$)

Following Back, Cao, and Willard (2000) "Imperfect Competition among Informed Traders", the presence of multiple insiders ($N > 1$) fundamentally alters the equilibrium.

### 4.1 Competition and Information Revelation

When $N$ insiders possess correlated (but imperfect) signals regarding $v$:

- **The Rat Race**: Early in the period, insiders aggressively trade to capitalize on their information before others do, leading to an initially steep rate of information revelation.
- **The Waiting Game**: As the price incorporates shared information, traders' value estimates diverge relative to the current price, causing them to take opposing positions later in the trading period.
- **Market Depth**: In a continuous-time equilibrium, market depth $1/\lambda(t)$ is no longer constant. It rises initially (encouraging delayed trading) but inevitably collapses to complete illiquidity as $t \to 1$ due to extreme adverse selection.

### 4.2 Formulating the Koopman Expansion

To extend our framework to $N$ insiders:

1. **Information Filtration**: Each Insider $j \in \{1,\dots,N\}$ has unique private information $\mathcal{F}_t^j$ and maintains their own belief hierarchy $\mathbf{Z}_{t,j}^{(k)}$.
2. **The Joint Order Flow**: The MM filters $Y_t = Z_t + \sum_j X_{t,j}$. In the Koopman Rationality paradigm, the MM's operator must account for the aggregated trading policies of all $N$ insiders.
3. **The N-Player HJB System**: Each Insider $j$ solves an HJB conditioned on their expectation of the MM's pricing _and_ the other $N-1$ insiders' trading strategies.

By defining the equilibrium under the Koopman framework, the complex coupled non-linear HJBs described by Back et al. transform into a system of coupled Linear-Quadratic games in the lifted signature space. This drastically simplifies the numerical search for the Nash Equilibrium of the continuous-time multi-agent game.

### 4.3 The Mean Field Game (MFG) Limit ($N \to \infty$)

As the number of informed traders $N$ becomes large, the computational burden of simulating $N$ coupled SDREs grows extensively. At this point, it is incredibly powerful to take the **Mean Field Limit**.
Instead of tracking $N$ individual competitors, the Insider responds to the _mean field_ (the probability distribution of the other agents' beliefs and states).

- **The Koopman Synergy**: Koopman Operator Theory is inherently designed to evolve observable functions over state spaces, naturally aligning with the Fokker-Planck (Kolmogorov Forward) equations governing the Mean Field distribution.
- **The MFG Equilibrium**: The game reduces to finding a representative Insider who solves their HJB optimally against the Mean Field, while the Mean Field itself is generated by everyone applying that optimal HJB policy.

While finite $N$ explicitly captures oligopolistic strategic interactions (e.g., specific market corners), the Mean Field Game formulation is theoretically elegant and often computationally _easier_ to solve for massive markets. We will contrast the finite $N$ coupled system against the MFG limit.

This fundamentally bridges Game Theory (Kyle/Back) with Koopman Operator Theory: **The immense complexity of the nonlinear game theory equilibrium is rendered tractable by shifting the equilibrium definition from the native nonlinear variable space to the linear Hilbert (Koopman) space.**
